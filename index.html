Champéry Workshop

Hotel Suisse • February 15–21, 2026

Dates: Sun 15 → Fri 21
Venue: Hotel Suisse (Champéry)
Format: talks + discussions

This workshop bring together researchers at the interface of machine learning, statistical physics, and theoretical computer science, with a focus on neural networks and high-dimensional computation. The goal is to foster deep, informal scientific exchanges around fundamental questions such as: how neural networks learn representations, why optimization exhibits phase transitions, and what limits computation and generalization in high dimensions.

Evening discussions

Evenings are devoted to open discussions organized around questions proposed during the workshop. Themes and groups will be announced on site.

  • Monday — Flash presentations (≈3 min)
  • Tuesday — Discussion session A
  • Wednesday — Discussion session B
  • Thursday — Discussion session C
  • Friday — Discussion session D

Current pool of discussion questions (may evolve):

  • Generalization in self-supervised learning: how should we properly define and measure it? (test loss can be flawed; “absence of memorization” can be flawed; what else?)
  • Models for self-supervised learning: what are good theoretical models to analyze it? (random language models? spiked cumulant models? what else?)
  • Population loss vs SGD speed: can we formulate a general theorem linking population loss to the speed of SGD? (aiming for an overarching statement beyond specific proofs)
  • Memorization taxonomy: which kinds of memorization matter, and what do we want to know? (harmful: diffusion models/privacy; beneficial: Hopfield-like, rules vs facts)
  • Sequence models beyond transformers: what alternatives are promising, and why do we tokenize data in the first place?
  • Neural scaling laws for (two-layer) nets: can we build a model that includes the number of parameters? and a counterpart for multi-layer learning?
  • Quenched Kac-Rice: can we prove the quenched Kac Rice formula?
  • Fine-tuning, continual learning, in-context learning: is fine-tuning crucial long-term, or will continual updates make it obsolete? is in-context learning overrated or fundamental?
